1804
Introduction

In February 1804, a Welsh mining engineer named Richard Trevithick lit the furnace of a machine nobody quite believed would move. It did — hauling ten tonnes of iron and seventy onlookers five miles through the Merthyr Tydfil colliery.

The world had not yet noticed. Within a generation, it could think of nothing else.

Railways compressed geography, dissolved isolation, and yanked the entire northern hemisphere into industrial modernity. A journey that took a week by carriage took hours by train. Empires were built along rail lines. Cities grew or died by whether the tracks came through.

Two centuries on, the train remains the most civilised way to move between places — and perhaps the most necessary.

A brief history of the
iron road

1804
Trevithick's Locomotive

The first full-scale steam locomotive runs at Merthyr Tydfil, Wales. Nobody calls it historic yet. Everyone calls it extremely loud.

1829
Stephenson's Rocket

The Rainhill Trials prove steam traction commercially viable. Rocket wins at 47 km/h. The age of railways — the age of speed — has arrived.

1830
First Passenger Railway

The Liverpool and Manchester Railway opens — the world's first inter-city passenger line with a fixed timetable, steam haulage throughout, and actual ticket offices.

1869
Transcontinental Railroad

The golden spike at Promontory, Utah. The American continent is sewn together. It took six years, tens of thousands of labourers, and one very famous photograph.

1883
Orient Express

Paris to Istanbul in four days. Mahogany panelling, six-course dinners, silver candlesticks. The Orient Express invents luxury rail travel and glamorises it for a century.

1938
Mallard's Record

LNER Class A4 No.4468 Mallard reaches 202.8 km/h on the East Coast Main Line. The absolute world steam speed record. It has never been broken.

1964
Shinkansen

Japan opens the Tōkaidō Shinkansen for the Tokyo Olympics at 210 km/h. In 60 years of service, carrying over ten billion passengers: zero fatalities.

2003
Maglev Goes Commercial

Shanghai Transrapid opens between Pudong Airport and the city. Thirty kilometres in seven minutes. The train floats on invisible magnetic fields. No wheels touch the rail.

Today
Hydrogen & Beyond

Alstom's Coradia iLint emits only steam. Hyperloop pods promise 1,000 km/h in vacuum tubes. The railway, two centuries old, keeps reinventing itself entirely.

Six journeys worth
taking your time over

Paris — Istanbul
The Orient Express

Inaugurated in 1883, this service became synonymous with luxury, espionage, and a particular brand of European mystery. Agatha Christie set her most famous novel aboard it; the real train surpassed even that glamour.

Since 1883
London — Edinburgh
LNER Mallard

On 3 July 1938, Mallard hauled a dynamometer test run to 202.8 km/h. The world steam speed record, set in eight minutes of controlled terror near Grantham. It has never been challenged.

202.8 km/h — 1938
Tokyo — Osaka
Tōkaidō Shinkansen

The world's busiest high-speed line has run since 1964 with zero passenger fatalities. Over ten billion journeys. It departs within thirty seconds of schedule. On average. Every day.

Since 1964 · 10B+ journeys
Moscow — Vladivostok
Trans-Siberian Railway

Nine thousand two hundred and eighty-nine kilometres. Eight time zones. Seven days. Taiga, tundra, the vast grey mirror of Lake Baikal glimpsed through a cold morning window. Still the greatest overland journey on earth.

Threading the Sacred Valley of the Incas at altitude, through cloud forest and along the churning Urubamba river, to arrive at one of the ancient world's most dramatic surviving sites. The journey earns the destination.

Widely considered the most luxurious train on the planet — a rolling Edwardian hotel. Antique brass, en-suite showers, open observation decks, the sound of the African night pressing in from every side.

Rovos Rail

"Railways have rendered more services and done more good than almost any other institution in the world."

— Robert Stephenson, 1850

The train occupies a peculiar position in human feeling — it is simultaneously nostalgic and progressive, industrial and ecological, public and intimate. No other form of travel places you so precisely in the landscape: not above it, not sealed from it, but moving through it at the speed at which it can be understood.

That quality is not incidental. The railway was always the democratic transport — long before the motorway, long before the budget airline, the train connected ordinary people to cities, to work, to each other. For much of the nineteenth century, it was the only way for a working person to move more than twenty miles in a day.

In the decades ahead, as aviation faces its carbon reckoning, the railway stands to reclaim the central role it held before the automobile arrived to dislodge it. High-speed lines are extending. Hydrogen trains are running. Night trains are returning to routes abandoned in the 1990s. The iron road turns out to be very long indeed.
